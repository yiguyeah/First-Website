Māori Culture Photo Gallery

The image portrayed above shows the traditional Māori dance of the Haka, involving loud singing and the slapping of thighs. This traditional dance is often seen at cultural festivals as well as sporting events such as the All Blacks.
Tā Moko, a traditional Māori tattoo, holds significant cultural significance to Māori heritage. This engraving originated from Polynesian Roots, and serves as the wearer's social status as well as their unique story.
A hāngī is a traditional Māori method of cooking food—specifically meat, root vegetables, and sometimes desserts—using heated rocks buried in a pit oven. It is a cornerstone of Māori culture, often used for large community gatherings, celebrations, and special occasions.
Māori flax weaving, known as raranga (plaiting) and whatu (finger-weaving), is a traditional art form utilising harakeke (New Zealand flax, Phormium tenax) to create practical and treasured items. It is a vital cultural practice that holds significant spiritual, social, and functional importance in Māori culture.
A Māori Marae is a sacred, fenced complex acting as a place for ceremonies, traditional rituals, or meetings. Furthermore, in relations to Māori culture, it fosters community connection and preserves Māori language and traditions.
Matariki serves as a day embarking the Māori New Year, characterised by 9 shining stars in night sky. It is a time for remembering those who have passed, celebrating the present with family and friends, and planning for the future. Moreover, it also holds a significant role in Māori mythology and folk tales with many stories being created reflecting on Matariki.
